I used to think leadership meant being the loudest person in the room. During sophomore year, I ran for student council because I thought that title would prove I was capable. I lost, and at first I felt embarrassed because it confirmed what I feared most: maybe I was not the kind of person other people naturally followed. After that election, I stopped trying to be visible and started paying attention instead. When our debate team struggled to retain new members, I noticed that most freshmen left because they felt intimidated. Practices moved too quickly, and no one wanted to admit they were confused. I started staying after meetings to help them one by one, explaining cases in plain language and showing them how I prepared rebuttals. That small habit changed more than the team. I realized leadership was not something I claimed in front of people. It was something I built by making people feel capable beside me. By junior year, I was elected captain, but the title mattered less than the trust behind it. Debate taught me that influence does not begin with authority. It begins with attention, patience, and the willingness to help someone else find their voice.

I used to think leadership belonged to the loudest person in the room. That belief followed me into sophomore year, when I ran for student council convinced that a title would prove I was capable. When I lost, the embarrassment hit harder than I expected. To me, it felt like evidence that I simply was not someone people wanted to follow. What changed me was not another campaign. It was what I noticed afterward. On our debate team, new members kept drifting away after only a few practices. Most were not uninterested; they were overwhelmed. Cases moved quickly, experienced debaters spoke in shorthand, and freshmen rarely admitted when they were lost. I began staying after practice to work with them one-on-one, translating arguments into plain language and walking them through how I built rebuttals. That routine taught me more about leadership than the election ever could.
